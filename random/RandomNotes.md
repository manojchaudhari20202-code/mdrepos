


Cross-domain architecture governance ensures secure, compliant, and consistent data flow between different security, business, or technology domains. It utilizes frameworks for monitoring performance, managing risks, and enforcing policies across, for example, high- and low-sensitivity networks using hardware diodes and software filters. Key aspects include establishing trust, implementing "block-all" by default, and maintaining architectural consistency. [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8]  
Key Components and Principles 

• Secure Information Exchange: Cross-Domain Solutions (CDS) (https://owlcyberdefense.com/learn-about-cross-domain-solutions/) facilitate the movement of data between systems with different sensitivity levels (e.g., classified vs. unclassified) while preventing unauthorized access. 
• Architectural Control: Governance requires defining, implementing, and monitoring the architecture to align with business goals. This includes overseeing cross-domain microservices to ensure they are properly integrated, monitored, and compliant. 
• Risk Management: Governance involves identifying and mitigating risks associated with data transfer, ensuring that security policies are consistently enforced across the entire system. 
• Technical Implementation: Solutions often involve a mix of hardware and software, such as data diodes that allow one-way data flow, preventing data leakage. [1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9]  

Governance Framework and Best Practices 

• Standards Adherence: Adhering to established principles from organizations like the NCSC and NSA (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rPwgaM_uCiw). 
• Role Definition: Defining clear roles for technical leaders to ensure consistent output, manage dependencies, and review architecture across domains. 
• Automation: Utilizing "architecture-as-code" (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BwgnZzkFot0) to monitor and direct architectural work. 
• Validation: Ensuring that cross-domain authentication solutions are secure, even when operating across independent, non-sharing domains. [7, 9, 10, 11]  

Common Challenges 

• Data Spills: Preventing the unauthorized transfer of data between, or leakage from, networks of different security levels. 
• Complexity: Managing the complexity of integrating different systems while maintaining high security, especially with high-throughput data like video. 
• Maintaining Autonomy: Allowing individual domains to retain operational control while participating in a larger, governed ecosystem. [2, 7, 12]
